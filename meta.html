Summary
Smartphone apps have become a very important part of everyday life and in succession mental health apps have garnered a lot of interest in the public and scientific community. Multiple meta-analyses have demonstrated that mental health apps treat symptoms of depression and anxiety modestly efficiently. However, despite the potential of these apps, a critical challenge remains: sustaining user engagement over time. Real-world data reveals that many users fail to maintain consistent engagement with mental health apps, with a median 15-day retention rate as low as 3.9%.

Wu. et al., conducted a systematic review and meta-analysis of the literature on smartphone apps for depression and anxiety. The objective was to assess the influence of persuasive design and behavioral economics techniques on both engagement and clinical outcomes.
This meta-analysis examines the impact of persuasive system design (PSD) and behavioral economics techniques on user engagement and clinical outcomes of smartphone apps for the treatment of depression and anxiety. The introduction highlights the widespread problem of low user engagement with mental health apps and emphasizes the potential benefits of engagement features to improve symptom relief.

The research method consisted of a systematic review according to PRISMA guidelines, focussing on published studies evaluating smartphone apps for depression and anxiety. Inclusion criteria included English-language empirical studies addressing the treatment of depression and/or anxiety by smartphone apps. The results of 25 eligible studies, including both randomized controlled trials and non-randomised studies, were evaluated. Overall, 4159 people took part in all the studies, 2905 of whom used different smartphone apps. A total of 29 unique smartphone apps with different intervention durations and control conditions were analyzed. The content of the apps mainly included behavioral techniques, cognitive techniques and psychoeducation.
The meta-analysis of the effect of smartphone apps on depressive or anxiety symptoms indicated a moderate positive effect size in reducing symptoms by comparing the score on a depression and anxiety questionnaire before and after using the app. The Meta-regression analysis shows, in comparison to control conditions, that there is a significant positive relationship with the amount of PSD features used in the apps and also with the different amount of PSD categories.

However, a significant negative relationship was found between the number of PSD features as well as categories and the study completion rate, suggesting that while engagement features enhance app efficacy, they may negatively impact completion rates.

Interestingly, even though these engagement techniques made the apps more effective, they also seemed to make people less likely to complete the study. The reasons for this are not clear and suggest that more research is needed to understand how to design apps that both help users and keep them engaged until the end.
Magnitude: How substantial are the effects?
Overall, the meta-analysis shows a significant positive effect with a calculated effect size of g = 0.62 for the reduction of depression and anxiety symptoms through smartphone apps. This effect size suggests that people who use these apps experience a significant improvement in symptoms. The observed effect size of g = 0.62 indicates a moderate positive effect, which implies that smartphone apps have significant efficacy in reducing depressive and anxiety symptoms. This result is consistent across other studies included in the meta-analysis, indicating a robust effect size estimate. However, it is important to note the substantial heterogeneity between studies (Q(df=24)= 82.70%), which reflects the diversity of study designs, participant characteristics and intervention protocols. Generality: How generalisable are the findings?
The meta-analysis includes data from 25 studies with 4159 participants total. The age span ranged from 13 to 76 years, with an average age of 32.9. This wide age range and the reasonable sample size represent a diverse population. However, the majority of participants were female (66.6%), which may limit the generalizability of the findings to male or nonbinary populations.

Additionally, the inclusion of both randomized controlled trials and non-randomized trials, could affect the validity of the results. The studies mainly focused on participants with MDD or generalized anxiety disorder, limiting the applicability of the results to these conditions individually and especially to other mental health disorders. The studies in the meta- analysis are covering a variety of treatment approaches, from behavioral and cognitive techniques to relaxation and mindfulness. These variations in app features and engagement strategies mixing the results of 29 different apps, may impact their generalisability, since these treatment approaches probably differ in effectiveness across different user demographics.

While the findings offer valuable insights into the efficacy of smartphone apps in treating MDD and anxiety disorders, it is important to be careful when considering their generalizability.

By interpreting the results, we have to keep in mind that the effectiveness of smartphone apps in treating mental health issues may vary due to the individuality of the apps and their features, the differences in treatment approaches they use, and the unique needs and conditions of each person dealing with mental health issues.

Interestingness: Is the meta-analysis relevant?
The meta-analysis is relevant, since it not only analyzes the efficacy of smartphone applications in treating symptoms of MDD and anxiety disorders but also shows the critical role of engagement features in these outcomes. This Meta-analysis highlights the potential of PSD features, suggesting that expanding the use of these features in mental health apps may increase clinical benefits. However, it also shows that further research is needed to understand the optimal integration of these features to also achieve more engagement.

By interpreting the results, we must remember that most of the population nowadays uses smartphones, and finding effective therapy can be extremely difficult as well. It's crucial to make treatments for disorders like depression and anxiety as accessible and non-intimidating as possible, especially since these conditions are often linked to challenges in seeking help and difficulties in interacting with others. Therefore, the effectiveness of smartphone apps might vary due to the distinct features of each app, the various treatment methods they employ, and the specific needs and conditions of individual users.

Credibility: How credible are the findings?
The method of this meta-analysis is closely aligned with established reporting standards such as the PRISMA guidelines and ensures transparency and completeness in study selection and data synthesis. The study protocol has been registered with PROSPERO, which enhances the credibility of the review process by providing a clear framework for conducting and reporting the analysis. The steps in the selection of primary studies and data extraction are well documented and transparent and reflect a systematic approach.
Therapists:
This meta-analysis provides important insights into the effectiveness of smartphone apps for the treatment of depression and anxiety disorders. The results show that apps with persuasive design features (PSD) and behavioral economics can achieve greater clinical benefit. In particular, the use of a variety of engagement techniques is associated with greater clinical impact. These findings suggest that integrating PSD and behavioral economics techniques into app development may improve treatment effectiveness. Therapists could benefit from this meta-analysis by looking for apps with effective design features when selecting apps for their patients.

Patients:
This meta-analysis highlights the effectiveness of smartphone apps in the treatment of depression and anxiety. Apps that use certain design features and behavioral economics techniques can help to effectively reduce symptoms. In particular, apps with a variety of engagement techniques can provide greater benefits. If someone is looking for an app to support your mental health, look out for ones that are based on compelling design principles and behavioral economics. These apps could help achieve treatment goals and improve symptoms.

Take-Home-Message: Choosing smartphone apps that focus on compelling design and behavioral economics techniques can improve treatment outcomes for depression and anxiety. Both therapists and patients can benefit from these findings by focusing on app features that maximize the engagement and effectiveness of digital mental health interventions.

Future Research:
Future research in this area could explore several important directions. First, further studies are needed to understand the specific mechanisms by which persuasive design (PSD) and behavioral economic techniques influence user engagement and clinical outcomes of mental health apps. Furthermore, investigating the optimal combination of these features to individual user characteristics could improve the effectiveness of apps. In addition, investigating the long-term impact and sustainability of app features that promote engagement beyond the intervention period would provide valuable insights for the development of digital mental health solutions that are designed to be scalable and durable. Finally, comparative effectiveness studies that directly evaluate different combinations of engagement strategies in real-world settings could support the development of evidence-based guidelines for the development and selection of effective mental health apps.
