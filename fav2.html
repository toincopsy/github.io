Moberg, C., Niles, A., & Beermann, D. (2019). Guided self-help works: randomized waitlist controlled trial of Pacifica, a mobile app integrating cognitive behavioral therapy and mindfulness for stress, anxiety, and depression. Journal of medical Internet research, 21(6), e12556.

Summary
In the study by Moberg, Niles and Beermann (2019), the researchers investigated the effectiveness of the Pacifica smartphone application, as a self-help intervention for individuals suffering from mild to moderate symptoms of stress, anxiety and depression. They used a randomized, controlled waiting list study to do so. Given the increasing prevalence of mental illness and the widespread use of mobile technology, this study explores the potential of Pacifica to provide accessible and effective mental health support.
The study design compared two groups: an immediate intervention group (Pacifica) with 253 participants and a waiting list control group (WL) with 247 participants. So a total of 500 participants were recruited. Participants were randomly assigned to one of the two groups and assessed on the basis of self-assessments of stress, anxiety, depression and self-efficacy. The Pacifica group was given access to the app for one month, which corresponds to the real-life use of the app, while the WL group had access to the intervention after a one-month waiting period. The immediate intervention group was reassessed two months after completion of the one-month intervention to assess the stability of symptom change. Only 101 WL participants and 79 participants in the active group completed post-assessments.

The measurements were made using validated scales such as the GAD-7 scale and the PHQ-8 scale. Overall, the study aimed to determine whether the Pacifica app effectively reduces symptoms of anxiety and depression compared to a control group with a waiting list under real-life conditions.
The results showed that participants using the Pacifica app had a significant reduction in depressive symptoms (PHQ-8) after treatment compared to participants in the waitlist control group. A higher rate of clinically significant changes in anxiety symptoms (GAD-7) was also observed in the Pacifica group compared to the waiting list. Of note, the benefits of Pacifica treatment were maintained at follow-up, with significant rates of clinically meaningful change in anxiety symptoms (48.9%) and depression symptoms (35.4%).
