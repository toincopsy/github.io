Guided Self-Help Works: Randomized Waitlist Controlled Trial of Pacifica, a Mobile App Integrating Cognitive Behavioral Therapy and Mindfulness for Stress, Anxiety, and Depression

Christine Moberg, Andrea Niles, Dale Beermann (2019)

Summary
In the study by Moberg, Niles and Beermann (2019), the researchers investigated the effectiveness of the Pacifica smartphone application, as a self-help intervention for individuals suffering from mild to moderate symptoms of stress, anxiety and depression. They used a randomized, controlled waiting list study to do so. Given the increasing prevalence of mental illness and the widespread use of mobile technology, this study explores the potential of Pacifica to provide accessible and effective mental health support.
The study design compared two groups: an immediate intervention group (Pacifica) with 253 participants and a waiting list control group (WL) with 247 participants, resulting in a total of 500 recruited participants. Participants were randomly assigned to one of the two groups and assessed using self-assessments of stress, anxiety, depression, and self-efficacy.

The Pacifica group was provided access to the app for one month, with no required level of use to mimic real-world app usage. Meanwhile, the WL group had access to the intervention after a one-month waiting period. Participants in both groups were assessed at two time points: baseline (pre treatment), 4 weeks later (post treatment; end of the intervention period). A follow-up assessment was completed only by the Pacifica group 3 months after the baseline/2 months post intervention (follow-up).

The measurements were made using validated scales such as the The measurements were made using validated scales such as the GAD-7 scale, the DASS-21, the PHQ-8 scale, the and the General Self-Efficacy Scale. Two composite measures were calculated from the depression scales (PHQ-8 and DASS-21 Depression Subscale) and anxiety scales (GAD-7 and DASS-21 Anxiety Subscale). Participant engagement was also assessed based on the frequency with which they logged into the app and the number of activities completed.
Symptoms: The analysis revealed significant interactions, indicating greater changes in the Pacifica group than the WL group. Significant time changes were found within the Pacifica group from pre-intervention to post-intervention while no significant changes within the Pacifica group were observed from post-intervention to the follow-up. However, significant group differences were found post-treatment, with the WL group showing higher scores for depression, anxiety, stress and lower self-efficacy compared to the Pacifica group.

Engagement: Although, no relationship between overall engagement and symptom improvement could be found, individuals who engaged in a higher number of thought records, a unique activity within the app, showed a positive impact on reducing anxiety and stress symptoms.

In summary, the Pacifica app demonstrated significant improvements in depression, anxiety, stress, and self-efficacy compared to the waitlist control group, with unique activities such as thought records showing promising effects on anxiety and stress reduction.
